## The Uncomfortable Truth About Middle Management

Let me start with something nobody tells you when you accept that "Head of Engineering" or "Engineering Manager" title:

**You just became the person everyone can blame, with the least power to change anything.**

You're squeezed between:
- **Executives** who want faster delivery, lower costs, and fewer excuses
- **Engineers** who want better tools, clearer requirements, and less bullshit
- **Product** who wants everything yesterday
- **Sales** who promised features that don't exist
- **Customers** who are angry about bugs you didn't write

And here's the kicker: **you don't have the authority to fix any of it unilaterally.**

You can't hire without approval. Can't change priorities without buy-in. Can't skip that useless meeting because "it's important to stakeholders." Can't tell Sales to stop overpromising because you're "not customer-facing."

Yet when delivery slips, when quality drops, when engineers quitâ€”**guess who's accountable?**

You.

Welcome to middle management. The hardest job in tech that nobody prepares you for.

## The Trap: Middle Management Without Real Power

Let's define the problem clearly.

### What Middle Management Is Supposed To Be

**In theory:**
- You set technical direction
- You manage team performance
- You control resources and priorities
- You shield your team from chaos
- You have autonomy within your domain

**In practice:**
- Product sets direction (you "influence")
- HR manages performance (you "recommend")
- Finance controls budget (you "request")
- Everyone adds to your backlog (you "negotiate")
- You have responsibility without authority

This is the fundamental tension: **you're accountable for outcomes you don't fully control.**

### The Symptoms of Powerless Middle Management

You know you're in this trap when:

**Decision Paralysis**
- Every technical decision requires 3 stakeholder meetings
- You can't approve a $500 tool purchase without VP sign-off
- Changing a sprint goal takes a change request board meeting

**Firefighting Mode**
- Your calendar is 90% reactive meetings
- You haven't written code in 6 months
- "Strategic planning" means next week's tasks
- You're always one emergency away from chaos

**Visibility Theater**
- You spend more time explaining work than doing work
- Status reports are your primary output
- Executives don't know what your team actually does
- Recognition goes to people above and below you, never to you

**Team Frustration**
- Engineers complain about blockers you can't remove
- "Why can't we just..." (but you can't change it)
- Good people leave because you can't fix culture issues
- You're the messenger getting shot

Sound familiar? You're not alone.

## The Stakeholder Juggling Act: Managing "Los Quioscos"

In LATAM, we have a saying: you're running multiple kiosks (quioscos). Each stakeholder is their own shop with different demands, and you're supposed to satisfy all of them simultaneously.

### Mapping Your Quioscos

**Quiosco 1: Engineering Team**
- **What they want:** Clear requirements, good tools, career growth, autonomy
- **What they complain about:** Changing priorities, technical debt, meetings
- **Your leverage:** Direct relationship, daily interaction, technical credibility
- **Your constraint:** No control over budget, headcount, or company priorities

**Quiosco 2: Product Leadership**
- **What they want:** Feature delivery, predictability, no surprises
- **What they complain about:** "Engineering is too slow," technical jargon, pushback
- **Your leverage:** Technical knowledge they lack, reality checks
- **Your constraint:** They control roadmap and priorities

**Quiosco 3: Executive Leadership**
- **What they want:** Business outcomes, cost efficiency, low drama
- **What they complain about:** Delays, budget overruns, engineers they don't understand
- **Your leverage:** You translate tech to business impact
- **Your constraint:** They control resources and strategy

**Quiosco 4: Peers (Sales, Marketing, Customer Success)**
- **What they want:** Their problems solved first, fast turnarounds
- **What they complain about:** "Engineering doesn't understand the business"
- **Your leverage:** Being responsive builds goodwill
- **Your constraint:** Their emergencies aren't your roadmap

**Quiosco 5: HR / People Ops**
- **What they want:** Performance reviews, culture initiatives, diversity goals
- **What they complain about:** Managers not doing 1-on-1s, documentation gaps
- **Your leverage:** Direct feedback on what actually works
- **Your constraint:** Process compliance isn't optional

### The Balancing Strategy

You can't satisfy everyone. Here's how to prioritize:

**1. Protect Your Team First**

This is non-negotiable. If your team burns out, nothing else matters.

```
Priority hierarchy:
1. Team health and sustainability
2. Delivery commitments
3. Stakeholder happiness
4. Long-term technical health
5. Your own visibility/career
```

**Practical example:**
- Sales demands urgent feature for "huge deal"
- It breaks sprint commitments
- Team already at capacity

**Bad response:** "Yes, we'll do it" (team burns out, quality drops)

**Good response:** "Yes, we can do it. What should we deprioritize from the current sprint? And we'll need to extend the timeline for X to avoid burnout."

You're not saying noâ€”you're making trade-offs explicit.

- **Other Engineering Managers:** Share strategies, vent safely
- **Executive Sponsor:** Find one person upstairs who has your back

**Why this matters:** When you need to push back on unreasonable demands, allies provide air cover.

Example:
- Product wants impossible deadline
- Your Product ally says "That's aggressive, what's the risk?"
- Now it's a conversation, not you being "difficult"

## Keeping Teams Motivated When You Can't Fix Everything

Here's the brutal reality: **your team sees the same dysfunction you do**, and they expect you to fix it.

But you can't. You can't get them the budget for that tool. You can't remove the useless meetings. You can't change the culture overnight.

So how do you keep them motivated when you're fighting battles you can't win?

### What Doesn't Work

**Toxic Positivity**
- "Everything is great!"
- "Just trust the process!"
- "The company really values you!"

Your team isn't stupid. They know it's bullshit.

**Learned Helplessness**
- "Nothing I can do about it"
- "That's just how it is here"
- "I agree but my hands are tied"

This destroys trust. You become just another corporate mouthpiece.

**Hero Complex**
- "I'll fix everything personally"
- Working 70-hour weeks to shield the team
- Taking all the heat yourself

You burn out. Team loses you. Everyone loses.

### What Actually Works

**1. Radical Transparency**

Be honest about constraints:

"I fought for that tool budget. We didn't get it this quarter because Finance is prioritizing X. I'm escalating for next quarter. In the meantime, here's a workaround."

**Why it works:** People accept limitations they understand. They resent hidden bureaucracy.

**2. Small Wins**

You can't change the company. But you can:
- Get approval for a $100/month tool that saves 5 hours/week
- Remove one useless meeting
- Implement "no-meeting Fridays" for deep work
- Celebrate shipped features publicly
- Get someone promoted

**Why it works:** Momentum beats grand promises.

**3. Autonomy Within Constraints**

"We have to build feature X. But HOW we build it is up to you. Propose the architecture. Own it. I'll remove blockers."

**Why it works:** People tolerate imposed goals if they control execution.

**4. Career Development**

When you can't give raises or promotions (because finance/HR controls it):
- Create technical mentorship programs
- Send people to conferences (fight for budget)
- Let them lead projects beyond their current level
- Give public credit constantly
- Write detailed performance reviews that help their next job

**Why it works:** Good people stay if they're growing, even if unhappy with company.

**5. Protect Their Time**

This is your actual superpower. You CAN:
- Say no to meetings on their behalf
- Push back on interrupt-driven culture
- Block off focus time
- Shield them from politics

**Why it works:** Time is the most valuable resource. You're giving them what they can't get elsewhere.

### The Motivation Framework

Motivation comes from three sources (per Dan Pink's "Drive"):

**1. Autonomy** - Control over their work
**2. Mastery** - Getting better at something meaningful
**3. Purpose** - Work matters

You can influence all three:

| Motivation Factor | What You CAN Control | What You CAN'T Control |
|------------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| **Autonomy** | Architecture decisions, how work gets done, team rituals | Roadmap priorities, company policies |
| **Mastery** | Learning budget, mentorship, stretch projects | Formal training programs (need budget) |
| **Purpose** | Connect work to impact, share customer stories | Company mission, product direction |

**Focus on what you CAN control.**

## Sustaining Performance Without Burning Out Your Team (Or Yourself)

High performance isn't about grinding harder. It's about **sustainable intensity**.

### The Anti-Burnout Strategies

**1. Ruthless Prioritization**

Your team can't do everything. Stop pretending they can.

**Framework: Must/Should/Could**
```
Must Do: (Top 3 this quarter)
- Feature X (customer contract dependent)
- Infrastructure Y (prevents outage)
- Hire 2 senior engineers

Should Do: (Next 5, if capacity allows)
- Refactor Z (reduces tech debt)
- Improve monitoring
- ...

Could Do: (Backlog, revisit next quarter)
- Nice-to-have features
- Process improvements
- ...
```

**Communicate this publicly.** When new requests come in, ask: "Where does this rank versus our current Must/Should/Could?"

**2. Protect Depth Work**

Your engineers need uninterrupted focus. Meetings kill this.

**Rules I enforce:**
- No meetings before 10am or after 3pm (local time)
- No meetings on Fridays
- Maximum 4 hours of meetings per week per engineer
- 1 "deep work day" per week (async only)

**3. Sustainable On-Call**

On-call rotation burns people out fastest.

**Fixes:**
- Rotate fairly (including you)
- Compensate properly (time off after incidents)
- Reduce false alerts aggressively (alert fatigue is real)
- Blameless postmortems (improve system, don't punish people)
- Clear escalation paths (don't leave juniors drowning alone)

**4. Explicit Recovery Time**

After crunch periods (launches, incidents):
- Mandate time off
- Reduce meeting load
- Assign low-stakes work
- Don't immediately start next crunch

**Analogy:** Athletes taper before big events and recover after. Engineers need the same.

**5. Lead by Example**

If you work weekends, skip lunch, and never take vacation, your team will too.

**Model healthy behavior:**
- Take your vacation (all of it)
- Leave at reasonable hours
- Don't send Slack messages at 11pm
- Admit when you're overwhelmed
- Ask for help

### Your Own Sustainability

You can't pour from an empty cup. Middle management is exhausting. **Protect yourself.**

**Time Management:**
- Block "manager time" vs. "maker time" on calendar
- Decline meetings without clear agendas
- Batch 1-on-1s on same days
- Have "no-interruption" blocks for strategic thinking

**Energy Management:**
- Delegate everything that doesn't require your unique skills
- Automate reporting (scripts for metrics, dashboards)
- Have a peer support group (other managers who get it)
- Therapy isn't weaknessâ€”it's maintenance

**Career Insurance:**
- Keep coding (even just side projects)
- Stay visible in industry (blog, conferences, open source)
- Build network outside your company
- Have "fuck-you money" saved (6-12 months runway)

**Why:** If you burn out or get fired, you need options.

## When to Stay, When to Leave

Middle management is hard. But is YOUR situation salvageable?

### Stay If:
- âœ… You have at least one executive sponsor who fights for you
- âœ… Your team respects you and sees you adding value
- âœ… You're learning skills that transfer (people management, strategy)
- âœ… Small wins are possible and accumulating
- âœ… Compensation is fair for the stress
- âœ… Company trajectory is positive (growing, profitable, not dying)

### Leave If:
- âŒ You've been blocked on every initiative for 6+ months
- âŒ Your team is actively undermining you or quitting
- âŒ Leadership blames you for structural problems
- âŒ You're being asked to do unethical things
- âŒ Your health is deteriorating (sleep, anxiety, depression)
- âŒ You've lost all sense of agency

**Critical test:** "If I left tomorrow, would my team be worse off?"

If yes: You're adding value. Stay and fight the good fight.

If no: You're a scapegoat or ineffective. Leave with dignity.

## Practical Tactics: The Middle Management Survival Kit

## The Long Game: Building Real Power

You might not have formal authority, but you can build **earned authority**:

### 1. Become The Go-To Person

- **For Product:** "Engineering lead who understands business"
- **For Engineers:** "Manager who actually fights for us"
- **For Execs:** "Leader who delivers and communicates clearly"

**How:** Consistent delivery, clear communication, solving problems without drama.

### 2. Expand Your Domain

Start small, prove value, expand:
```
Year 1: Manage 1 team (5 engineers)
Year 2: Manage 2 teams (12 engineers)
Year 3: Manage engineering org (30 engineers, 3 managers)
```

**Each expansion gives you more leverage** (bigger budget, more headcount, seat at table).

### 3. Build Alliances Beyond Your Boss

Your direct manager might not have power either. Build relationships with:
- CTO / VP Engineering
- CEO (if <100 person company)
- Board members (if you can)
- Investors (for startups)

**Not to go around your boss**â€”to build organizational capital.

### 4. Create Forcing Functions

Change is hard. Create structure that forces good behavior:

- **Architecture Review Board:** Forces technical decisions to be documented
- **Weekly Ship Emails:** Forces visibility of progress
- **Monthly Tech Talks:** Forces knowledge sharing
- **Quarterly Planning:** Forces alignment with business goals

**These become "just how we work"** and give you structural power.

### 5. Develop Leaders Below You

Your best path to Director/VP is having people who can replace you.

**Intentionally:**
- Delegate high-visibility projects
- Let senior engineers lead meetings
- Mentor future managers
- Give credit publicly

**When your team succeeds without you, you've earned the promotion.**

## Conclusion: It's The Hardest Job, But It Matters

Middle management is brutal. You're:
- Accountable without authority
- Squeezed between competing interests
- Expected to motivate teams through chaos
- Often invisible to leadership
- First to blame when things fail

**And yet, you matter more than anyone.**

You're the:
- **Shock absorber** between executive chaos and engineering execution
- **Translator** between business goals and technical reality
- **Multiplier** of team effectiveness
- **Shield** protecting your team from dysfunction
- **Bridge** connecting work to purpose

**Without good middle managers, companies collapse.**

**My advice:**

1. **Accept the constraints.** You'll never have enough power. Work within the system.
2. **Focus on small wins.** You can't fix everything, but you can improve something.
3. **Protect your team.** Their health and growth are your primary responsibility.
4. **Build visibility.** Your work is invisible unless you make it visible.
5. **Sustain yourself.** Burnout helps no one.
6. **Know when to leave.** Some situations are unfixable.

**But most importantly:** Remember why you took the role.

If it was just the title or money, you'll hate it.

If it's because you believe good leadership makes teams better, you'll find meaning even in the chaos.

The balance is hard. But it's achievable.

And the teams you build, the people you develop, the culture you createâ€”**that's the legacy that outlasts any title.**
